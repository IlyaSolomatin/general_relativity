# Vectors

## A vector exists before any axes

A vector is an arrow. It has a length and a direction, and nothing else.

<VectorArrow />

<Callout type="insight" title="The claim">
  A vector exists on its own, before you draw any axes or set up coordinates.
</Callout>

Consider a displacement: *"walk 100 steps toward that mountain."* The
instruction means something even if no one has drawn a grid on the ground. The
grid is something we add afterward. The walk is real without it.

## Choosing axes gives it components

To calculate with the arrow, we need numbers. So we lay down axes.

Draw two perpendicular axes, $x$ (east) and $y$ (north) — the familiar straight,
perpendicular $x$–$y$ grid, called a **Cartesian** coordinate system. Take the
arrow *"3 steps east, 4 steps north."* Against these axes we read off two numbers:

- along $x$:  $\;3$
- along $y$:  $\;4$

These two numbers, $(3, 4)$, are the **components** of the vector in this
coordinate system.

<Callout type="note" title="Definition">
  The **components** of a vector are the numbers that describe it once you have
  chosen axes — its readings along each axis.
</Callout>

The picture below shows the arrow, the axes, and its two components.

<VectorFigure />

So there are two separate things:

- **the vector** — the arrow itself;
- **its components** $(3, 4)$ — a description that depends on the axes you drew.

## Different axes, different components

The components are the arrow's readings against the axes *you chose*. Choose
different axes and you read different numbers — for the same arrow.

Our arrow is "3 east, 4 north," so in the east–north axes its components are
$(3, 4)$. Now pick new axes whose $x$-axis points straight along the arrow. The
arrow lies entirely on the new $x$-axis: it has some length along it and nothing
across it. Its length is $5$ (a 3–4–5 right triangle), so in these axes its
components are $(5, 0)$.

So $(3, 4)$ and $(5, 0)$ describe the identical arrow. The arrow did not change;
only the axes we measured it against did.

Rotate the axes below and watch the components change while the arrow stays put.
The rotation here keeps the axes perpendicular, but that is not required — axes
can also be tilted unevenly or scaled. Rotation is just the easiest case to see.

<RotatingAxes />

## The axes as vectors: basis vectors

So far the axes have been directions to measure against. Now we describe them
with vectors. Along each axis, place a unit arrow — an arrow of length 1 pointing
that way:

- $e_x$: length 1, pointing east
- $e_y$: length 1, pointing north

<VectorWithBasis />

These two arrows are the **basis vectors**. Our arrow "3 east, 4 north" is then
built from them:

$$A = 3\,e_x + 4\,e_y$$

Read it literally: take 3 copies of $e_x$ and 4 copies of $e_y$, lay them tip to
tail, and you reach the tip of $A$.

<BasisVectorsFigure />

<Callout type="note" title="Definition">
  The **basis vectors** are unit arrows along the chosen axes. A vector's
  **components** are the amounts of each basis vector needed to build it — here,
  3 of $e_x$ and 4 of $e_y$.
</Callout>

<Callout type="insight" title="The basis is a choice">
  Basis vectors and the coordinate grid are separate things: the grid labels
  *points*, a basis decomposes *vectors*. We **chose** $e_x, e_y$ as unit arrows
  along the axes — the grid's own **coordinate basis** (the arrow for a one-unit
  step in each coordinate). That choice is exactly why a vector's components come
  out equal to its grid coordinates. A stretched basis (say $|e_x| = 2$) or a
  tilted one would describe the same arrow with different components.
</Callout>

## Changing the axes is changing the basis

We want to be able to move from one set of axes to another — that is, to change
the basis vectors — and to understand how the same vector will be written in
different bases. "Choosing new axes" and "choosing new basis vectors" are the same move. Take the
arrow again:

- Old basis $e_x$ (east), $e_y$ (north):  $A = 3\,e_x + 4\,e_y$.
- New basis $e_{x'}$, $e_{y'}$. Let it be, for example, a unit arrow along $A$, and a unit arrow perpendicular to it:  $A = 5\,e_{x'} + 0\,e_{y'}$.

The arrow never moved. The basis vectors and the components changed *together*,
so the combination still equals the same $A$:

$$3\,e_x + 4\,e_y \;=\; 5\,e_{x'} + 0\,e_{y'} \;=\; A$$

<NewBasisFigure />

The components are free to change only because the basis changed underneath them,
holding $A$ fixed.

## The general rule for changing from one basis to another

Take any new basis — two <Term def="A set of vectors is independent when
none of them is a linear combination of the others — equivalently,
the only combination equal to zero is the trivial one:
  " math="c_1 v_1 + \cdots + c_n v_n = 0 \implies c_1 = \cdots =
  c_n = 0">independent</Term> vectors $e_{x'}, e_{y'}$.
Each is some combination of the old basis:

$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= a\,e_x + b\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= c\,e_x + d\,e_y
\end{aligned}
$$

The numbers $a, b, c, d$ are almost arbitrary. The only requirement is that
$e_{x'}$ and $e_{y'}$ are not parallel, which also means that
$ad - bc \neq 0$ (see the exercise below if you wonder why).
Parallel $e_{x'}$ and $e_{y'}$ do not form a basis.

<Spoiler title="Exercise: prove the equivalence of vector indepence statements">

Given the new vectors in old coordinates, $e_{x'} = (a, b)$ and $e_{y'} = (c, d)$,
show that the following three statements say exactly the same thing:

1. **Independent** — the only solution of $\alpha\,e_{x'} + \beta\,e_{y'} = 0$ is $\alpha = \beta = 0$.
2. **Not parallel** — neither vector is a scalar multiple of the other.
3. $ad - bc \neq 0$.

<Spoiler title="Solution">

**Independent $\iff ad - bc \neq 0$.**

In components, $\alpha\,e_{x'} + \beta\,e_{y'} = 0$ is the system

$$
\begin{aligned}
a\,\alpha + c\,\beta &= 0 \\
b\,\alpha + d\,\beta &= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

Eliminate $\beta$ — multiply the first equation by $d$, the second by $c$, and
subtract:

$$(ad - bc)\,\alpha = 0 .$$

Eliminate $\alpha$ the same way (first by $b$, second by $a$, subtract):

$$(ad - bc)\,\beta = 0 .$$

If $ad - bc \neq 0$, these force $\alpha = \beta = 0$ — only the trivial solution,
so the vectors are independent. If $ad - bc = 0$, then the coefficients
$(\alpha, \beta) = (c, -a)$
solve both equations: the first gives $ac - ca = 0$, the second gives
$bc - ad = -(ad - bc) = 0$. That is a nontrivial relation unless $a = c = 0$, and
in that leftover case both vectors have the form $(0,\,\cdot)$ — multiples of
$e_y$, hence dependent anyway. So $ad - bc = 0$ forces dependence. Therefore
independent $\iff ad - bc \neq 0$.

**Independent $\iff$ not parallel.**
If the pair is *dependent*, some $\alpha, \beta$ not both zero give
$\alpha\,e_{x'} + \beta\,e_{y'} = 0$; whichever coefficient is nonzero lets us
write one vector as a multiple of the other, so they are parallel. Conversely, if
$e_{y'} = \lambda\,e_{x'}$, then $\lambda\,e_{x'} - e_{y'} = 0$ is a nontrivial
relation, so the pair is dependent. Thus dependent $\iff$ parallel, and taking
negations, independent $\iff$ not parallel.

Putting the two equivalences together, all three conditions are the same.

<Callout type="insight" title="A geometric reading">
  $ad - bc$ is the signed **area** of the parallelogram spanned by $e_{x'}$ and
  $e_{y'}$. That area is zero exactly when the parallelogram collapses to a line —
  when the vectors are parallel. So "$ad - bc \neq 0$" literally says the two
  vectors enclose a nonzero area, which is the same as pointing in different
  directions.
</Callout>

</Spoiler>

</Spoiler>

Collect the coefficients $a, b, c, d$ into a matrix $S$ whose
columns are the new basis vectors written in old coordinates:

$$S = \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix}$$

The arrow is one object in either basis:

$$A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y \;=\; A_{x'}\,e_{x'} + A_{y'}\,e_{y'}$$

Substitute the new basis vectors into the right-hand side and gather the $e_x$
and $e_y$ terms:

$$A = (a\,A_{x'} + c\,A_{y'})\,e_x + (b\,A_{x'} + d\,A_{y'})\,e_y$$

Does it remind you of how $A$ is written in the old basis,
$A = A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y$?
Exactly. Because an independent basis gives a *unique* expansion, the two
expressions must share the same coefficients — so we can read the old components
straight off, e.g. $A_{x} = a\,A_{x'} + c\,A_{y'}$.
Or in matrix form,

$$
\begin{bmatrix} A_x \\ A_y \end{bmatrix}
= S \begin{bmatrix} A_{x'} \\ A_{y'} \end{bmatrix}
\qquad (\text{old} = S \cdot \text{new}).
$$

And $S$ is invertible precisely because the new vectors form a basis
($ad - bc \neq 0$). Solving for the new components:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} A_{x'} \\ A_{y'} \end{bmatrix}
= S^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} A_x \\ A_y \end{bmatrix}
\qquad (\text{new} = S^{-1} \cdot \text{old}).
$$

The components transform by the inverse, $S^{-1}$.

Consider a simple example: if we just stretch each basis vector to twice its length,
$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= 2\,e_x + 0\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= 0\,e_x + 2\,e_y,
\end{aligned}
$$

this will mean $a = d = 2, \,b = c = 0$, or equivalently
$S = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.
Then $S^{-1}  = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} \end{bmatrix}$,
and the components halve — the basis doubled, the components halved: exactly the
inverse, as $S^{-1}$ predicts.

<ChangeOfBasis />

Take the vector with old components $(3, 5)$. Build a new basis by rotating the

its length. In old coordinates the new basis vectors are

$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\cos 30°\,e_x + \sin 30°\,e_y\big) = \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4}\,e_x + \tfrac{1}{4}\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= \tfrac{1}{2}\big(-\sin 30°\,e_x + \cos 30°\,e_y\big) = -\tfrac{1}{4}\,e_x + \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4}\,e_y
\end{aligned}
$$

<RotatedBasis />

Find the components of the vector in this new basis.

<Spoiler title="Solution">

**Build $S$.** Its columns are the new basis vectors written in old coordinates:

$$

**Invert.** Using a formula for inverting 2x2 matrix $\begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -c \\ -b & a \end{bmatrix}$,

## The length of a vector

Imagine a physical ruler of unit length. We can lay it against the arrow to
measure how long it is. But can we calculate that length (designated $|A|$) from the vector's
components instead?

Yes. The immediate idea is the Pythagorean theorem: for components $(A_x, A_y)$,

$$|A| = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}.$$

This is a good choice for an *orthonormal* basis — where the basis vectors have
unit length and form a right angle — but it will not work in other bases. To
adjust the calculation, we need to know the lengths of the basis vectors and the
angle between them.

## The dot product

The tool that carries that information is the **dot product**. It takes two vectors
and returns a number:

$$A \cdot B = |A|\,|B|\cos\theta,$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between them.

Set $B = A$: a vector makes angle $0$ with itself and $\cos 0 = 1$, so

$$A \cdot A = |A|^2, \qquad\text{hence}\qquad |A| = \sqrt{A \cdot A}.$$

Dot product is linear in each slot — it distributes over sums and lets scalars out:

$$(A + B)\cdot C = A\cdot C + B\cdot C, \qquad (kA)\cdot B = k\,(A\cdot B),$$

and likewise in the second slot.

<DotProduct />

## The metric

Write $A$ in a basis and dot it with itself, using linearity in both slots:

$$A \cdot A = (A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y) \cdot (A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y) = A_x^2\,(e_x\cdot e_x) + 2\,A_x A_y\,(e_x\cdot e_y) + A_y^2\,(e_y\cdot e_y).$$

(The two cross terms merge because $e_x\cdot e_y = e_y\cdot e_x$.) Every coefficient
is a dot product of basis vectors. Name them:

$$g_{xx} = e_x\cdot e_x,\qquad g_{xy} = e_x\cdot e_y,\qquad g_{yy} = e_y\cdot e_y.$$

Then

<Eq label="length">

$$|A|^2 = g_{xx}\,A_x^2 + 2\,g_{xy}\,A_x A_y + g_{yy}\,A_y^2.$$

</Eq>

<Callout type="note" title="The metric">
  The **metric** is the set of dot products among the basis vectors —
  $g_{xx} = e_x\cdot e_x$, $g_{xy} = e_x\cdot e_y$, $g_{yy} = e_y\cdot e_y$. It is
  what turns components into true lengths: $g_{xx} = |e_x|^2$ and $g_{yy} = |e_y|^2$
  are the basis vectors' squared lengths, and $g_{xy} = |e_x|\,|e_y|\cos\theta$
  carries the angle between them.
</Callout>

Two checks:

- **Orthonormal basis** ($g_{xx} = g_{yy} = 1$, $g_{xy} = 0$): the formula collapses to $|A|^2 = A_x^2 + A_y^2$ — plain Pythagoras.
- **A stretched basis**, with $e_x$ of length $2$ and $e_y$ of length $1$, perpendicular ($g_{xx} = 4$, $g_{yy} = 1$, $g_{xy} = 0$): a vector with components $(1.5, 4)$ has $|A|^2 = 4\,(1.5)^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25$, so $|A| = 5$. The naive $\sqrt{1.5^2 + 4^2} \approx 4.27$ would be wrong; the metric gives the true $5$.

A careful reader may ask: "wait, but to calculate the length of a vector, we also
need to know lengths of basis vectors? Where do we get it from?" Indeed, to measure lengths of any kinds and distances we need
to have a notion of a unit length. It can be introduced as a "physical ruler" like
we did in chapter "The length of a vector", or as an orthonormal basis where we
agree to call the length of the basis vector a unit length. This is the only "calibration"
we need to perform to be able to calculate lengths of any vectors in any bases.

## The metric gives angles too

Length was the case $B = A$. The same expansion works for the dot product of *any*
two vectors. With $A = A_x e_x + A_y e_y$ and $B = B_x e_x + B_y e_y$, linearity
gives

$$A \cdot B = g_{xx}\,A_x B_x + g_{xy}\,(A_x B_y + A_y B_x) + g_{yy}\,A_y B_y.$$

The same three numbers $g_{xx}, g_{xy}, g_{yy}$. So the metric computes the dot
product of any two vectors from their components — and once you have dot products,
you have angles:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{A \cdot B}{|A|\,|B|}.$$

So the metric is the complete geometry machine for the space: feed it two vectors'
components and it returns lengths (set $B = A$) and angles. Every distance and
every angle is encoded in $g_{xx}, g_{xy}, g_{yy}$.

## The metric may change from point to point

So far we used one basis for the whole plane, so the metric was a single constant
table. Generally speaking, it is not necessarily the case.

Use **polar coordinates**: label each point by its distance from the center $r$
and its angle $\theta$. We can choose the basis vectors for any point in the following way:

- $e_r$ — the step that increases $r$, pointing outward. Moving out by $dr$ always covers distance $dr$, so $|e_r| = 1$ everywhere.
- $e_\theta$ — the step that increases the angle $\theta$. Increasing $\theta$ by a small $d\theta$ moves you along an arc of length $r\,d\theta$: near the center a degree of angle is a tiny move, far out the same degree is a large one. So the per-angle step has length $r$, growing as you go outward.

<Callout type="insight" title="Why this basis?">
  Remember, we are free to pick any basis at each point. We pick *this* one because it is the
  coordinate basis of the polar grid — the one in which a small coordinate shift *is*
  its own decomposition. Move by $(dr, d\theta)$ and the displacement is
  $dr\,e_r + d\theta\,e_\theta$, so its components are exactly the coordinate steps
  $(dr, d\theta)$. That is what fixes the lengths: $e_r$ is one unit of distance
  ($|e_r| = 1$), and $e_\theta$ is the arc that one unit of angle sweeps
  ($|e_\theta| = r$).
</Callout>

<PolarGrid />

Reading off the metric (dot products of the basis vectors):

$$g_{rr} = |e_r|^2 = 1, \qquad g_{\theta\theta} = |e_\theta|^2 = r^2, \qquad g_{r\theta} = e_r\cdot e_\theta = 0.$$

So $g_{\theta\theta} = r^2$: the metric is genuinely different at different points —
small near the center, large far out. **The metric changes from point to point.**

And so does the basis. For the Cartesian grid, $e_x$ and $e_y$ were the same arrows
at every point — one basis served the whole plane. Here they are not: $e_r$ and
$e_\theta$ point in different directions, and $e_\theta$ has a different length, at
every point. A curvilinear grid does not carry one fixed basis; it carries a basis
*at each point* — a field of basis vectors.

<Callout type="insight" title="The changes compensate">
  Both the basis and the metric change from point to point. But the metric is just
  the dot products of the local basis, $g_{ij} = e_i\cdot e_j$, so the components
  and the metric always change together. In the length formula
  <EqRef label="length" /> their changes cancel, and it gives the true length at
  every point.
</Callout>

A careful reader may object: "earlier we decomposed a vector into basis
vectors, but now the basis is defined only at a point, while a vector reaches
across space, through many different local bases. So how do we decompose a vector
at all?"

The instinct behind this worry is exactly right, and it becomes the full story
once space is genuinely curved (which comes later). But here, on the flat plane in
polar coordinates, nothing is actually lost.

The plane is still flat, so a large arrow between two distant points is still a
perfectly good vector — you can add such arrows and scale them exactly as before.
What changed is only that the coordinate basis $(e_r, e_\theta)$ differs from point
to point, so there is no single "polar basis" to expand a large arrow in. But you
are not stuck: flat space always admits a fixed basis (e.g. Cartesian), and any large
vector can be decomposed there — or you may freeze the polar basis at a single
chosen point and use it, since it is still a valid basis of the whole flat plane,
even though it won't be linked to the polar coordinate system anymore.
The position-dependent basis still works for local, tiny vectors though.

## The line element

Everything so far measured one straight arrow $A$. But most geometry is about the
distance *along a path* — and about spaces whose geometry changes from place to
place, as we just saw. The metric handles both through one idea: measure a tiny
step, then add up the steps.

A tiny step is just a very short vector, with components $(dx, dy)$. Its squared
length is the metric applied to it:

$$ds^2 = g_{xx}\,dx^2 + 2\,g_{xy}\,dx\,dy + g_{yy}\,dy^2.$$

$ds$ is the **line element** — the length of an infinitesimal step. The length of
a whole path is the sum (integral) of $ds$ along it.

Two things this unlocks that a single straight arrow could not:

1. **Paths can curve** — lay down tiny straight steps along any path and add their lengths.
2. **The metric can vary from point to point** — each step is measured by the local metric where it sits.
