# Vectors

## A vector exists before any axes

A vector is an arrow. It has a length and a direction, and nothing else.

<VectorArrow />

<Callout type="insight" title="The claim">
  A vector exists on its own, before you draw any axes or set up coordinates.
</Callout>

Consider a displacement: *"walk 100 steps toward that mountain."* The
instruction means something even if no one has drawn a grid on the ground. The
grid is something we add afterward. The walk is real without it.

## Choosing axes gives it components

To calculate with the arrow, we need numbers. So we lay down axes.

Draw two perpendicular axes, $x$ (east) and $y$ (north). Take the arrow *"3 steps
east, 4 steps north."* Against these axes we read off two numbers:

- along $x$:  $\;3$
- along $y$:  $\;4$

These two numbers, $(3, 4)$, are the **components** of the vector in this
coordinate system.

<Callout type="note" title="Definition">
  The **components** of a vector are the numbers that describe it once you have
  chosen axes — its readings along each axis.
</Callout>

The picture below shows the arrow, the axes, and its two components.

<VectorFigure />

So there are two separate things:

- **the vector** — the arrow itself;
- **its components** $(3, 4)$ — a description that depends on the axes you drew.

## Different axes, different components

The components are the arrow's readings against the axes *you chose*. Choose
different axes and you read different numbers — for the same arrow.

Our arrow is "3 east, 4 north," so in the east–north axes its components are
$(3, 4)$. Now pick new axes whose $x$-axis points straight along the arrow. The
arrow lies entirely on the new $x$-axis: it has some length along it and nothing
across it. Its length is $5$ (a 3–4–5 right triangle), so in these axes its
components are $(5, 0)$.

So $(3, 4)$ and $(5, 0)$ describe the identical arrow. The arrow did not change;
only the axes we measured it against did.

Rotate the axes below and watch the components change while the arrow stays put.
The rotation here keeps the axes perpendicular, but that is not required — axes
can also be tilted unevenly or scaled. Rotation is just the easiest case to see.

<RotatingAxes />

## The axes as vectors: basis vectors

So far the axes have been directions to measure against. Now we describe them
with vectors. Along each axis, place a unit arrow — an arrow of length 1 pointing
that way:

- $e_x$: length 1, pointing east
- $e_y$: length 1, pointing north

<VectorWithBasis />

These two arrows are the **basis vectors**. Our arrow "3 east, 4 north" is then
built from them:

$$A = 3\,e_x + 4\,e_y$$

Read it literally: take 3 copies of $e_x$ and 4 copies of $e_y$, lay them tip to
tail, and you reach the tip of $A$.

<BasisVectorsFigure />

<Callout type="note" title="Definition">
  The **basis vectors** are unit arrows along the chosen axes. A vector's
  **components** are the amounts of each basis vector needed to build it — here,
  3 of $e_x$ and 4 of $e_y$.
</Callout>

## Changing the axes is changing the basis

We want to be able to move from one set of axes to another — that is, to change
the basis vectors — and to understand how the same vector will be written in
different bases. "Choosing new axes" and "choosing new basis vectors" are the same move. Take the
arrow again:

- Old basis $e_x$ (east), $e_y$ (north):  $A = 3\,e_x + 4\,e_y$.
- New basis $e_{x'}$, $e_{y'}$. Let it be, for example, a unit arrow along $A$, and a unit arrow perpendicular to it:  $A = 5\,e_{x'} + 0\,e_{y'}$.

The arrow never moved. The basis vectors and the components changed *together*,
so the combination still equals the same $A$:

$$3\,e_x + 4\,e_y \;=\; 5\,e_{x'} + 0\,e_{y'} \;=\; A$$

<NewBasisFigure />

The components are free to change only because the basis changed underneath them,
holding $A$ fixed.

## The general rule for changing from one basis to another

Take any new basis — two independent vectors $e_{x'}, e_{y'}$.
Each is some combination of the old basis:

$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= a\,e_x + b\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= c\,e_x + d\,e_y
\end{aligned}
$$

The numbers $a, b, c, d$ are arbitrary, subject only to the independence
condition $ad - bc \neq 0$. Otherwise $e_{x'}$ and $e_{y'}$ do not form a basis,
because one vector could be expressed in terms of the other (in our
two-dimensional case that would mean the two basis vectors are parallel).

Collect them as the matrix $S$ whose
columns are the new basis vectors written in old coordinates:

$$S = \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix}$$

The arrow is one object in either basis:

$$A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y \;=\; A_{x'}\,e_{x'} + A_{y'}\,e_{y'}$$

Substitute the new basis vectors into the right-hand side and gather the $e_x$
and $e_y$ terms:

$$A = (a\,A_{x'} + c\,A_{y'})\,e_x + (b\,A_{x'} + d\,A_{y'})\,e_y$$

Because the old basis is independent, the coefficients must match one for one.
In matrix form,

$$
\begin{bmatrix} A_x \\ A_y \end{bmatrix}
= S \begin{bmatrix} A_{x'} \\ A_{y'} \end{bmatrix}
\qquad (\text{old} = S \cdot \text{new}).
$$

And $S$ is invertible precisely because the new vectors form a basis
($ad - bc \neq 0$). Solving for the new components:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} A_{x'} \\ A_{y'} \end{bmatrix}
= S^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} A_x \\ A_y \end{bmatrix}
\qquad (\text{new} = S^{-1} \cdot \text{old}).
$$

No assumption entered beyond "it is a change of basis." The new basis vectors are
the columns of $S$; the components transform by the inverse, $S^{-1}$.

The two earlier results are just special cases:

- **Rotation** by $\varphi$: $S = \begin{bmatrix}\cos\varphi & -\sin\varphi \\ \sin\varphi & \cos\varphi\end{bmatrix}$, and $S^{-1}$ is rotation by $-\varphi$ — the rule from the previous section.
- **Doubling** the basis: $S = 2I$, so $S^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{2}I$ and the components halve.

<Callout type="note" title="Definition">
  Components that transform by the **inverse** $S^{-1}$ of the basis-change matrix
  $S$ are called **contravariant** ("contra" = against: they vary against the
  basis). Every arrow-type vector behaves this way. By convention they are marked
  with an upstairs index, $A^x$.
</Callout>

## Writing the new basis in the old

To find the rule connecting old and new components, we first connect the two
bases. The new basis vectors are arrows in the same plane, so each is a
combination of $e_x$ and $e_y$.

For example, let the new axes be the old ones rotated counterclockwise by an angle $\varphi$.
Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= \cos\varphi\,e_x + \sin\varphi\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= -\sin\varphi\,e_x + \cos\varphi\,e_y
\end{aligned}
$$

These are not pulled from a hat. $e_{x'}$ is a unit arrow at angle $\varphi$ from
the old $x$-axis, and a unit arrow at angle $\varphi$ has components
$(\cos\varphi, \sin\varphi)$ — that is the definition of cosine and sine.
Likewise $e_{y'}$ is a unit arrow at angle $\varphi + 90°$, giving
$(-\sin\varphi, \cos\varphi)$.

<RotatedBasis />

As a check, at $\varphi = 90°$ we have $\cos 90° = 0$ and $\sin 90° = 1$, so
$e_{x'} = e_y$ and $e_{y'} = -e_x$: a quarter-turn sends the new $x$-axis onto the
old $y$-axis, and the new $y$-axis onto the old $-x$.

## How the components change

We have the rule for the basis vectors. Now the real target: the components.

The arrow $A$ is the same object in either basis:

$$A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y \;=\; A_{x'}\,e_{x'} + A_{y'}\,e_{y'}$$

To compare the two sides, put everything in one basis. Write the old basis
vectors in terms of the new ones. The new axes are the old rotated by $+\varphi$,
so the old axes are the new rotated by $-\varphi$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
e_x &= \cos\varphi\,e_{x'} - \sin\varphi\,e_{y'} \\
e_y &= \sin\varphi\,e_{x'} + \cos\varphi\,e_{y'}
\end{aligned}
$$

(This is the previous pair read backwards — swap primed and unprimed and flip the
sign of $\varphi$ — or just solve those two equations for $e_x, e_y$.)

Substitute into $A = A_x e_x + A_y e_y$ and gather the $e_{x'}$ and $e_{y'}$ terms:

$$A = (A_x\cos\varphi + A_y\sin\varphi)\,e_{x'} + (-A_x\sin\varphi + A_y\cos\varphi)\,e_{y'}$$

Compare with $A = A_{x'} e_{x'} + A_{y'} e_{y'}$. Because $e_{x'}$ and $e_{y'}$ are
independent directions, the coefficients must match one for one:

$$
\begin{aligned}
A_{x'} &= A_x\cos\varphi + A_y\sin\varphi \\
A_{y'} &= -A_x\sin\varphi + A_y\cos\varphi
\end{aligned}
$$

That is the rule: from the old components and the angle $\varphi$, it returns the
new ones. It is exactly what the slider above computes — at $\varphi = 0$ it gives
$(3,4)$ back; at the angle that lines the $x$-axis up with $A$, it gives $(5,0)$.

<Callout type="insight" title="The pattern">
  The axes turned by $+\varphi$, but the component pair turned by $-\varphi$. The
  components rotate opposite to the axes.
</Callout>

</Spoiler>
