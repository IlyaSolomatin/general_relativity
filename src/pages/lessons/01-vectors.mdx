# Vectors

## A vector exists before any axes

A vector is an arrow. It has a length and a direction, and nothing else.

<VectorArrow />

<Callout type="insight" title="The claim">
  A vector exists on its own, before you draw any axes or set up coordinates.
</Callout>

Consider a displacement: *"walk 100 steps toward that mountain."* The
instruction means something even if no one has drawn a grid on the ground. The
grid is something we add afterward. The walk is real without it.

## Choosing axes gives it components

To calculate with the arrow, we need numbers. So we lay down axes.

Draw two perpendicular axes, $x$ (east) and $y$ (north). Take the arrow *"3 steps
east, 4 steps north."* Against these axes we read off two numbers:

- along $x$:  $\;3$
- along $y$:  $\;4$

These two numbers, $(3, 4)$, are the **components** of the vector in this
coordinate system.

<Callout type="note" title="Definition">
  The **components** of a vector are the numbers that describe it once you have
  chosen axes — its readings along each axis.
</Callout>

The picture below shows the arrow, the axes, and its two components.

<VectorFigure />

So there are two separate things:

- **the vector** — the arrow itself;
- **its components** $(3, 4)$ — a description that depends on the axes you drew.

## Different axes, different components

The components are the arrow's readings against the axes *you chose*. Choose
different axes and you read different numbers — for the same arrow.

Our arrow is "3 east, 4 north," so in the east–north axes its components are
$(3, 4)$. Now pick new axes whose $x$-axis points straight along the arrow. The
arrow lies entirely on the new $x$-axis: it has some length along it and nothing
across it. Its length is $5$ (a 3–4–5 right triangle), so in these axes its
components are $(5, 0)$.

So $(3, 4)$ and $(5, 0)$ describe the identical arrow. The arrow did not change;
only the axes we measured it against did.

Rotate the axes below and watch the components change while the arrow stays put.
The rotation here keeps the axes perpendicular, but that is not required — axes
can also be tilted unevenly or scaled. Rotation is just the easiest case to see.

<RotatingAxes />

## The axes as vectors: basis vectors

So far the axes have been directions to measure against. Now we describe them
with vectors. Along each axis, place a unit arrow — an arrow of length 1 pointing
that way:

- $e_x$: length 1, pointing east
- $e_y$: length 1, pointing north

<VectorWithBasis />

These two arrows are the **basis vectors**. Our arrow "3 east, 4 north" is then
built from them:

$$A = 3\,e_x + 4\,e_y$$

Read it literally: take 3 copies of $e_x$ and 4 copies of $e_y$, lay them tip to
tail, and you reach the tip of $A$.

<BasisVectorsFigure />

<Callout type="note" title="Definition">
  The **basis vectors** are unit arrows along the chosen axes. A vector's
  **components** are the amounts of each basis vector needed to build it — here,
  3 of $e_x$ and 4 of $e_y$.
</Callout>

## Changing the axes is changing the basis

We want to be able to move from one set of axes to another — that is, to change
the basis vectors — and to understand how the same vector will be written in
different bases. "Choosing new axes" and "choosing new basis vectors" are the same move. Take the
arrow again:

- Old basis $e_x$ (east), $e_y$ (north):  $A = 3\,e_x + 4\,e_y$.
- New basis $e_{x'}$, $e_{y'}$. Let it be, for example, a unit arrow along $A$, and a unit arrow perpendicular to it:  $A = 5\,e_{x'} + 0\,e_{y'}$.

The arrow never moved. The basis vectors and the components changed *together*,
so the combination still equals the same $A$:

$$3\,e_x + 4\,e_y \;=\; 5\,e_{x'} + 0\,e_{y'} \;=\; A$$

<NewBasisFigure />

The components are free to change only because the basis changed underneath them,
holding $A$ fixed.

## The general rule for changing from one basis to another

Take any new basis — two <Term def="A set of vectors is independent when
none of them is a linear combination of the others — equivalently,
the only combination equal to zero is the trivial one:
  " math="c_1 v_1 + \cdots + c_n v_n = 0 \implies c_1 = \cdots =
  c_n = 0">independent</Term> vectors $e_{x'}, e_{y'}$.
Each is some combination of the old basis:

$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= a\,e_x + b\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= c\,e_x + d\,e_y
\end{aligned}
$$

The numbers $a, b, c, d$ are almost arbitrary. The only requirement is that
$e_{x'}$ and $e_{y'}$ are not parallel, which also means that
$ad - bc \neq 0$ (see the exercise below if you wonder why).
Parallel $e_{x'}$ and $e_{y'}$ do not form a basis.

<Spoiler title="Exercise: prove the equivalence of vector indepence statements">

Given the new vectors in old coordinates, $e_{x'} = (a, b)$ and $e_{y'} = (c, d)$,
show that the following three statements say exactly the same thing:

1. **Independent** — the only solution of $\alpha\,e_{x'} + \beta\,e_{y'} = 0$ is $\alpha = \beta = 0$.
2. **Not parallel** — neither vector is a scalar multiple of the other.
3. $ad - bc \neq 0$.

<Spoiler title="Solution">

**Independent $\iff ad - bc \neq 0$.**

In components, $\alpha\,e_{x'} + \beta\,e_{y'} = 0$ is the system

$$
\begin{aligned}
a\,\alpha + c\,\beta &= 0 \\
b\,\alpha + d\,\beta &= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

Eliminate $\beta$ — multiply the first equation by $d$, the second by $c$, and
subtract:

$$(ad - bc)\,\alpha = 0 .$$

Eliminate $\alpha$ the same way (first by $b$, second by $a$, subtract):

$$(ad - bc)\,\beta = 0 .$$

If $ad - bc \neq 0$, these force $\alpha = \beta = 0$ — only the trivial solution,
so the vectors are independent. If $ad - bc = 0$, then the coefficients
$(\alpha, \beta) = (c, -a)$
solve both equations: the first gives $ac - ca = 0$, the second gives
$bc - ad = -(ad - bc) = 0$. That is a nontrivial relation unless $a = c = 0$, and
in that leftover case both vectors have the form $(0,\,\cdot)$ — multiples of
$e_y$, hence dependent anyway. So $ad - bc = 0$ forces dependence. Therefore
independent $\iff ad - bc \neq 0$.

**Independent $\iff$ not parallel.**
If the pair is *dependent*, some $\alpha, \beta$ not both zero give
$\alpha\,e_{x'} + \beta\,e_{y'} = 0$; whichever coefficient is nonzero lets us
write one vector as a multiple of the other, so they are parallel. Conversely, if
$e_{y'} = \lambda\,e_{x'}$, then $\lambda\,e_{x'} - e_{y'} = 0$ is a nontrivial
relation, so the pair is dependent. Thus dependent $\iff$ parallel, and taking
negations, independent $\iff$ not parallel.

Putting the two equivalences together, all three conditions are the same.

<Callout type="insight" title="A geometric reading">
  $ad - bc$ is the signed **area** of the parallelogram spanned by $e_{x'}$ and
  $e_{y'}$. That area is zero exactly when the parallelogram collapses to a line —
  when the vectors are parallel. So "$ad - bc \neq 0$" literally says the two
  vectors enclose a nonzero area, which is the same as pointing in different
  directions.
</Callout>

</Spoiler>

</Spoiler>

Collect the coefficients $a, b, c, d$ into a matrix $S$ whose
columns are the new basis vectors written in old coordinates:

$$S = \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix}$$

The arrow is one object in either basis:

$$A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y \;=\; A_{x'}\,e_{x'} + A_{y'}\,e_{y'}$$

Substitute the new basis vectors into the right-hand side and gather the $e_x$
and $e_y$ terms:

$$A = (a\,A_{x'} + c\,A_{y'})\,e_x + (b\,A_{x'} + d\,A_{y'})\,e_y$$

Does it remind you of how $A$ is written in the old basis,
$A = A_x\,e_x + A_y\,e_y$?
Exactly. Because an independent basis gives a *unique* expansion, the two
expressions must share the same coefficients — so we can read the old components
straight off, e.g. $A_{x} = a\,A_{x'} + c\,A_{y'}$.
Or in matrix form,

$$
\begin{bmatrix} A_x \\ A_y \end{bmatrix}
= S \begin{bmatrix} A_{x'} \\ A_{y'} \end{bmatrix}
\qquad (\text{old} = S \cdot \text{new}).
$$

And $S$ is invertible precisely because the new vectors form a basis
($ad - bc \neq 0$). Solving for the new components:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} A_{x'} \\ A_{y'} \end{bmatrix}
= S^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} A_x \\ A_y \end{bmatrix}
\qquad (\text{new} = S^{-1} \cdot \text{old}).
$$

The components transform by the inverse, $S^{-1}$.

Consider a simple example: if we just stretch each basis vector to twice its length,
$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= 2\,e_x + 0\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= 0\,e_x + 2\,e_y,
\end{aligned}
$$

this will mean $a = d = 2, \,b = c = 0$, or equivalently
$S = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.
Then $S^{-1}  = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} \end{bmatrix}$,
and the components halve — the basis doubled, the components halved: exactly the
inverse, as $S^{-1}$ predicts.

<Spoiler title="Exercise: components under a rotation and a squeeze">

Take the vector with old components $(3, 5)$. Build a new basis by rotating the
old one $30°$ counterclockwise and then squeezing each basis vector to **half**
its length. In old coordinates the new basis vectors are

$$
\begin{aligned}
e_{x'} &= \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\cos 30°\,e_x + \sin 30°\,e_y\big) = \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4}\,e_x + \tfrac{1}{4}\,e_y \\
e_{y'} &= \tfrac{1}{2}\big(-\sin 30°\,e_x + \cos 30°\,e_y\big) = -\tfrac{1}{4}\,e_x + \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4}\,e_y
\end{aligned}
$$

<RotatedBasis />

Find the components of the vector in this new basis.

<Spoiler title="Solution">

**Build $S$.** Its columns are the new basis vectors written in old coordinates:

$$
S = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4} & -\tfrac{1}{4} \\ \tfrac{1}{4} & \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4} \end{bmatrix}.
$$

**Invert.** Using a formula for inverting 2x2 matrix $\begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -c \\ -b & a \end{bmatrix}$,

$$
S^{-1} = 4\begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4} & \tfrac{1}{4} \\ -\tfrac{1}{4} & \tfrac{\sqrt3}{4} \end{bmatrix}
= \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt3 & 1 \\ -1 & \sqrt3 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

**Apply $S^{-1}$ to the old components** $(3, 5)$:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} A_{x'} \\ A_{y'} \end{bmatrix}
= \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt3 & 1 \\ -1 & \sqrt3 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}
= \begin{bmatrix} 3\sqrt3 + 5 \\ 5\sqrt3 - 3 \end{bmatrix}
\approx \begin{bmatrix} 10.20 \\ 5.66 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

</Spoiler>
